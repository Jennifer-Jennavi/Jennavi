Understanding
Your Hooks

What is a LinkedIn hook and why does it matter?
A LinkedIn hook is the first one or two lines of your post that appear before the See More button. It is the only part of your post that is visible without clicking. If the hook does not stop the scroll, nobody reads the rest. Over 80% of LinkedIn users never click See More on posts whose first line does not immediately interest them. Your hook is not just important. It is everything.
What are the four CRICKETS hook types?
Recognition hooks make the reader feel immediately seen and understood. They name a specific experience or frustration so precisely that the reader thinks you wrote the post about them. Discomfort hooks name a painful truth the reader has been avoiding. They create urgency by surfacing consequences the reader has been ignoring. Curiosity hooks create an information gap the reader feels compelled to close. They withhold something the reader wants to know. Surprise hooks challenge a belief the reader holds as true. They stop the scroll by contradicting an assumption.
Which hook type should I use most often?
The best LinkedIn content strategies rotate through all four types. Recognition builds connection and trust. Discomfort drives action and urgency. Curiosity generates clicks and saves. Surprise creates shares and debate. Using only one type makes your content predictable and your engagement will plateau. Jennifer's ghostwriting clients use all four in a structured rotation that keeps their audience engaged across every stage of the buyer journey.

Questions About Hook Auditing
A hook works when it creates an immediate emotional response before the reader has made a conscious decision to keep reading. That response is either recognition (this is about me), discomfort (I have been avoiding this), curiosity (I need to know the answer), or surprise (I did not expect that). A hook that triggers none of those four responses gets scrolled past regardless of how good the rest of the post is.
LinkedIn readers are moving fast. When a post opens with "I", the implicit message is that this post is about the writer, not the reader. That is the fastest way to lose the scroll. The most effective hooks open with the reader's situation, a number, a statement about their industry, or a challenge to something they believe. The writer can enter the post after the hook has done its job.
Under 200 characters. LinkedIn shows the first one to two lines before the See More button. Everything after that is hidden. Your hook needs to create enough tension or curiosity in those first two lines to make the reader click. A hook that runs longer than 200 characters is usually trying to do too much. The best hooks are one sentence that lands hard and stops there.
Scroll-Stop Power measures how immediately your hook creates an emotional or intellectual interruption. A hook that opens with a number, a contradiction, a painful truth, or a specific recognition earns high Scroll-Stop Power because it creates a break in the reader's pattern. A hook that opens with a general statement or a question like "Have you ever wondered..." earns low Scroll-Stop Power because it asks for effort before delivering anything.
